# How great brands build landing pages — 20-company study

Date: 2026-07-15. Studied the live homepages of 20 well-branded companies across five
fields to fix a first draft that read like a slide deck. Companies:
- **AI-native:** OpenAI, Anthropic, Perplexity, Cursor
- **Elite dev/infra:** Linear, Vercel, Stripe, Framer
- **Fintech/money:** Mercury, Wise, Brex, Plaid
- **Product/design brands:** Notion, Figma, Superhuman, Raycast
- **Iconic consumer:** Apple, Airbnb, Tesla, Oura

The agreement across all five fields was near-total. The findings below are not
one field's convention; they are what great pages do everywhere.

## The eight rules (near-unanimous across 20 companies)

1. **The hero is ONE claim — identity or outcome, never mechanics.** "Radically different
   banking" (Mercury). "iPhone" (Apple). "Agentic Infrastructure" (Vercel). "The intelligent
   canvas for infinite creativity" (Figma). None explains *how* it works in the headline.
   Total hero text is almost always under ~25 words; often far less.

2. **NO problem section. Anywhere. On any of the 20.** Zero pain-point setup, zero "tired of
   X," zero "the old way is broken." Every page assumes you know the problem and skips
   straight to identity or product. This was the most universal finding — our draft's
   "Paying vendors is still a manual job" section is exactly what nobody does.

3. **The strongest move is *no hero copy at all* — hand over the product.** OpenAI and
   Perplexity don't write a headline; they give you the input box. The product IS the page.
   The fix for over-explaining isn't better copy, it's less copy plus a live thing working.

4. **AI is flexed by DEMONSTRATION, never description.** Nobody writes "powered by advanced
   AI." OpenAI/Perplexity put the model in front of you; Cursor stacks live agent demos.
   Anthropic is the lone outlier and flexes *positioning* ("safety at the frontier"), not
   mechanics. Show it working; never claim it in adjectives.

5. **No "credibility strip" of trust chips.** Trust is handled two ways only: (a) ONE
   quantified social-proof number near the top ("Trusted by 35,000+ companies" — Brex;
   "Loved by 300K+ entrepreneurs" — Mercury), or (b) a dedicated, well-designed section
   further down (Mercury's "Standard protection stops short" → "$5M FDIC," framed as UPSIDE
   not fear). A row of little compliance chips is what nobody does. Our credibility strip
   dies here; self-custody becomes a real section.

6. **The product (or overwhelming proof) is the visual — not abstract art, not a busy dashboard as the first thing.** 
   Real UI, motion between real screens, or a live demo.
   Only Stripe/Plaid lean on brand gradients, and they can because everyone already trusts
   them. Pre-launch with no logos, our lever is: show the real product working.

7. **Density is INVERTED from a deck.** Sparsest at the top (one line, one CTA, sometimes
   zero words); detail lives *below the fold* as short title+visual pairs, never paragraphs.
   Our draft was dense at the top and that's backwards.

8. **Confidence reads as restraint; one bet, unhedged.** Flat declaratives, concrete numbers
   instead of adjectives ("under 20 seconds, 140+ countries" — Wise, not "fast and global"),
   no hype, no exclamation points. Max two CTAs, short and specific ("Deploy Now,"
   "Open account"), never "Learn more." Each page picks its single most convincing asset and
   leads with only that.

## Two field-specific lessons that matter for us

- **Cross-border, done right (Wise):** stated as a concrete, quantified, jargon-free claim in
  the hero itself — "the fast way to send money abroad… most arrive in under 20 seconds…
  140+ countries." And FX fairness explained by analogy to something already trusted ("the
  rate you can usually check on Google"), never by explaining the mechanism. If cross-border
  is core (it is), it shows up as a believable number, not as one capability among five.
- **Brex is our cautionary example:** the most catalog-like of all 20 — a wall of feature-tile
  section headlines ("Corporate cards," "Expense management," "Bill pay," "Banking")… which
  is exactly the per-pipeline-stage structure our first draft copied from the AP competitors.
  Treat it as the anti-pattern, not the model.

## What this changes for Decimal (supersedes STRUCTURE.md v1 + COPY.md v1)

- **Cut the problem section entirely.** (Rule 2.)
- **Cut the credibility strip.** (Rule 5.) Trust becomes a real, upside-framed section.
- **Flex the AI in the opening move**, by showing the product read/code/approve, not by
  saying "AI-powered." (Rule 4.) Zaid: "we're an AI company, flex it at the beginning."
- **Self-custody gets its own hero-level section** — it's a highlight feature, not a chip.
  Frame as upside ("your money never leaves your control"), Mercury-style, not fear.
- **Hero = one bold AI-forward claim + the real product as the visual + one CTA** (plus the
  "see the interactive demo" secondary). No explanatory paragraph up top.
- **Far fewer sections, each one bet, product-led, shown not told.** Not a block per stage.
- **Cross-border = one concrete quantified claim** shown in the product, Wise-style.
- Keep: house voice (plain, no em-dash, no crypto jargon), sell-the-dream-as-built, vendors-only,
  one unified product. See `POSITIONING-CORRECTED.md`.
