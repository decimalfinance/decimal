# Coupa (and SAP Concur) org-wide policy engines — research notes

Scope: this is about POLICY (spend rules, budget gates, match tolerances, receipt/vendor rules) not approval-chain ROUTING, which was researched separately. Goal: pull out what a 5-50 person AP product (Decimal) should steal in miniature from the enterprise gold standard.

## 1. What "policy" means in Coupa's world

Coupa doesn't have one single "policy engine" screen — policy is distributed across several subsystems that all feed the same idea: make the compliant purchase the easy purchase, and catch violations at the earliest possible moment (ideally before the PO is even cut, not after the invoice arrives).

The policy types that show up across Coupa's docs and marketing:

- **Spend/category policies** — rules tied to categories, cost centers, departments, or commodity codes (e.g. "travel over $X needs VP approval," "software purchases must go through IT category owner"). Coupa's procurement pages describe requests being "matched to catalog items, contracts, budgets, and approval rules" and approvals routed "by cost center, department, amount, or category." (https://www.coupa.com/products/procure-to-pay/procurement/, via search snippet — direct fetch of coupa.com blocked by their WAF)
- **Budget policies** — real-time budget consumption checks at requisition time, before the PO is issued. Budgets can be structured by cost center, location, commodity/category, and period, tracked against PO, invoice, and expense-report transactions. (https://compass.coupa.com/en-us/products/core-platform/integration-playbooks-and-resources/other-integration-playbooks/erp-integration-adapters/integration-scenarios/10.-budget-integration-scenarios)
- **PO policies / "no PO, no pay"** — a hard organizational rule that AP will not pay an invoice unless it references an approved PO created before delivery. This is a very common enterprise control pattern (not unique to Coupa — Coupa is often the tool used to enforce it). Exceptions are handled via blanket POs for recurring spend, expedited/emergency PO workflows, and P-cards for small transactions rather than by weakening the rule. (https://planergy.com/blog/no-po-no-pay-policy/, https://www.apog.com/supplier-portal/terms-conditions-supplier-code-conduct/no-po-no-pay-policy)
- **2-way / 3-way match + tolerances** — 2-way match compares invoice to PO (price/qty); 3-way match adds the goods receipt as the authoritative record of what was actually delivered, so invoice-to-receipt is the comparison that matters most once a receipt exists. Coupa auto-approves ("touchless") invoices that fall inside a configured tolerance band (a preset % or $ variance allowed between invoice and PO/receipt) and only escalates to a human when a matching failure exceeds tolerance — at which point it goes into a "tolerance hold" and AP works it with the requester/approver. (https://www.coupa.com/blog/what-is-invoice-matching/, https://www.medius.com/blog/what-is-a-3-way-match-versus-a-2-way-match/, https://www.zoneandco.com/glossary/3-way-match)
- **Receipt / receiving policies** — separate "receiving tolerances" govern how much a user is allowed to receive against a PO (over-receipt / under-receipt limits), independent of the invoice tolerance. This is Coupa modeling the physical-goods step as its own policy surface, not folding it into invoice matching. (https://get.coupa.com/rs/950-OLU-185/images/Receiving_Tolerances.pdf — PDF, not machine-readable via fetch, title/existence confirmed via search)
- **Vendor/supplier risk policies** — supplier risk scoring (financial health, news sentiment, fraud signals) feeds into requisition-time warnings: "upfront visibility into issues such as risk flags and potential duplicates from your supplier master before submitting each request." Coupa also runs compliance checks (certifications, labor/environmental compliance) as part of supplier onboarding and requisition intake, and can block invoice submission until a supplier provides required onboarding info. (https://www.coupa.com/products/source-to-contract/supplier-risk-performance/, https://www.zanovoy.com/blog-posts/streamline-your-supplier-onboarding-workflow-with-coupa-intake-management)

## 2. How policy is expressed and where it surfaces

Coupa's dominant UX pattern is **guided buying**: policy isn't a document employees are expected to read, it's baked into the intake form itself. Preferred suppliers, contracted items, and negotiated rates are surfaced by default so that compliance is the path of least resistance rather than a rule enforced after the fact. Real-time budget visibility is shown to the requester and approver at the point of request — "instantly understand the financial impact of requests before making decisions" — not after submission.

Underneath guided buying, Coupa's actual configuration primitives (from the docs ecosystem, e.g. Process Automator Triggers/Actions in Compass) are condition → action rules: a trigger (amount threshold, category, budget-remaining check, missing field) fires an action (route to approver, block submission, send a warning, auto-approve). This is a conditions/thresholds rules engine, not a hard-coded workflow — thresholds and category mappings are admin-configurable per org.

Routine, low-risk purchases that pass all policy checks are **auto-approved**; only requests above a threshold or that fail a check get routed to a human, and larger dollar amounts route to cross-functional/executive sign-off. (https://www.coupa.com/products/procure-to-pay/, https://www.coupa.com/blog/why-pre-approved-spend-ultimate-benchmark/)

## 3. Enforcement model: hard stop vs warn vs route-differently

Coupa itself doesn't publish a clean "red rule / yellow rule" taxonomy the way Concur does, but the pattern across its docs is the same three-tier model:
1. **Hard stop** — no PO, no pay is the clearest example: the system makes it structurally impossible to send an invoice through without a PO reference. Missing-supplier-onboarding-info can also block invoice submission entirely.
2. **Warn / flag, don't block** — tolerance-band mismatches, supplier risk flags, and duplicate-supplier warnings surface at requisition or invoice time as visibility, and let the process continue with the info attached to the record.
3. **Route differently** — the most common Coupa pattern. A violation (over-budget, out-of-tolerance, non-catalog item, unapproved category) doesn't block the transaction, it changes the approval path — adds an approver, escalates to a category owner, or drops the invoice into an AP exception queue for manual matching instead of touchless auto-pay.

**SAP Concur is much more explicit about this and is worth citing directly as the cleaner mental model**: Concur's audit rules are literally labeled by severity —
- **Red rules = hard stop.** Submission is blocked until the violation is resolved. Used for things like missing required receipts or amounts that exceed a hard spending limit.
- **Yellow rules = soft warning.** The system flags a possible policy violation and requires an explanation/justification, but the employee can still submit. Used where judgment/context matters more than a flat block.
- Flagged items are also surfaced to the approver/auditor at review time regardless of severity, so even yellow-rule items get downstream visibility.
- Concur explicitly warns about "alert fatigue" — too many yellow warnings and users start ignoring all of them, which is a real design constraint, not just a nice-to-have.
(https://concurfreelancer.com/2025/01/17/mastering-sap-concur-audit-rules/, https://help.sap.com/docs/CONCUR_TRAVEL/178215a2a80f49689f5b7909d51a0e9e/2fd317d8432c4b7b872a9d0a29cb82d4.html)

Concur's 2026 roadmap is also relevant context: they're moving toward **AI-generated policy rules from a natural-language policy document** — upload your travel/expense policy PDF and the system proposes audit rules, rather than an admin hand-authoring conditions. Concur Expense gets natural-language rule creation/editing in Q2 2026, and rule generation from uploaded policy docs later in 2026. This validates that even the enterprise leaders see manual rules-engine configuration as a UX problem worth automating away. (https://www.cpapracticeadvisor.com/2026/03/17/sap-launches-new-ai-integrated-travel-and-expense-enhancements/179875/, https://itbrief.co.uk/story/sap-concur-adds-ai-agents-deeper-card-travel-links)

## 4. Violation reporting / fraud monitoring (Coupa SpendGuard)

Separate from real-time enforcement, Coupa runs **SpendGuard**, a continuous anomaly/fraud-detection layer over spend data — described as covering "invoices, expenses, payments" with 25+ alerts across six categories of spending activity. It factors in things like address matches, approval-chain patterns, and timestamp anomalies to catch things a single-transaction rule wouldn't (e.g. a pattern of just-under-threshold requests, or an approver who is also the requester's manager approving unusually fast). This is a detective/monitoring control, distinct from the preventive controls (guided buying, budget checks, match tolerances) — it exists because preventive rules alone don't catch behavioral fraud patterns. (https://www.coupa.com/blog/managing-purchase-requisitions-spend-guard/, https://www.zanovoy.com/coupa-business-spend-management/coupa-modules/coupa-spend-guard — exact alert taxonomy not independently confirmed beyond the "25+ alerts / 6 categories" marketing claim, treat as directionally true not exact spec)

## 5. Budget integration specifics

- Budgets are modeled as their own object, structured along configurable segments of the chart of accounts (cost center, location/commodity, custom dimensions) and a period (monthly/quarterly/annual).
- Budget consumption is checked against transactions at multiple stages — requisition, PO, invoice, and expense report — not just once at approval. This means a budget can be "reserved" at requisition time before the PO is even cut, so two people can't both commit the last $10k of a budget line without the second one seeing it's gone.
- The exact enforcement (block vs warn vs extra-approver) at the budget layer isn't detailed in public docs (Coupa gates this behind their integration playbooks), but the general ERP pattern — confirmed by analogy to Dynamics 365 budget control — is configurable per org: "soft control" shows a warning and lets the user proceed, "hard control" blocks the transaction outright. Coupa almost certainly offers both, configurable per budget/category, given its general enforcement pattern elsewhere.

## 6. What's overkill for a 5-50 person company vs genuinely valuable at any size

**Genuinely valuable even at 5-50 people (steal these, miniaturized):**
- **No-PO-no-pay as a togglable hard rule**, scoped to whichever categories/vendors matter (e.g. "no invoice without a linked order/request" for anything over $X). This is cheap to implement and is the single highest-leverage AP control that exists — it's the difference between "we found the fraud in the audit" and "the fraud request never went anywhere."
- **Real-time budget-remaining visibility at request time**, even if enforcement is just a soft warning. Showing "$3,200 of $5,000 marketing budget left" on the intake form the moment someone drafts a request is high value, low engineering cost, and it's the single most-cited "aha" feature across guided-buying marketing.
- **Match tolerance bands with auto-approve inside, human review outside.** Even a flat $/% tolerance (e.g. "auto-clear if invoice is within 2% or $25 of the linked order") gets a small org most of the touchless-processing benefit without building a full 3-way-match engine.
- **Two-tier severity (hard stop vs soft warning)** — Concur's red/yellow model is simple to reason about and simple to build. Worth adopting as the enforcement vocabulary in Decimal's policy engine rather than inventing a third state.
- **Vendor-level flags surfaced at request time** (new vendor, no bank details on file, flagged as high-risk/duplicate) — cheap signal, prevents a large share of AP fraud (business email compromise / fake vendor payment redirects), which is disproportionately a small-company risk since they have fewer eyes on each payment.

**Overkill / not worth building at 5-50 people (skip or defer):**
- **Full 3-way match with a separate receiving/receipts subsystem.** Small companies rarely have a warehouse/receiving function; goods-receipt tracking as its own object is enterprise-manufacturing-shaped, not SMB-services-shaped. A lightweight "mark as received" checkbox tied to the order is enough; don't build separate receiving tolerances.
- **Category-specific rules engine with commodity-code-level granularity.** Enterprise category management assumes a large enough catalog and enough procurement staff to maintain taxonomy. A small org needs maybe 3-5 policy categories (travel, software, contractors, one-off vendor payments, everything else), not a commodity code tree.
- **Dedicated fraud-monitoring layer (SpendGuard-equivalent) with 25+ behavioral alerts.** This is a detective control that only pays for itself at high transaction volume where a human can't eyeball every payment. At 5-50 people, the review/approve/release pipeline itself (a human actually looking at each payment) is the fraud control — a bolt-on anomaly-detection system is solving a problem this org size doesn't have yet.
- **AI-generated policy from an uploaded policy PDF (Concur's 2026 direction).** Cool, but it's solving the problem of "we have a 40-page written policy and don't want to hand-transcribe it into rules." A small org's whole policy fits in a handful of toggles; there's no document to ingest.
- **Guided-buying catalog/punch-out integration** (steering users to pre-negotiated contracted items/suppliers). This assumes negotiated contracts and a curated catalog, which most 5-50 person companies don't have — their AP problem is "who did we pay and was it approved," not "did we buy off-contract."

## What Decimal should steal / avoid

Steal: (1) no-PO-no-pay as an org-level toggle scoped by amount/category, (2) budget-remaining shown live on the request form even before enforcement exists, (3) a flat tolerance band for auto-clearing small invoice/order mismatches instead of building full 3-way match, (4) Concur's red/yellow (hard-stop/soft-warn) severity model as the enforcement vocabulary for every policy type Decimal ships, (5) vendor-risk flags (new vendor, no bank-details-on-file, recently-changed bank details) surfaced right at request/review time — this is the single highest fraud-prevention-per-line-of-code item on this whole list.

Avoid: full receiving/receipt subsystems, commodity-code-level category rules, a dedicated anomaly-detection/fraud-scoring layer, AI policy-doc ingestion, and catalog/punch-out guided buying — all of these assume organizational scale (procurement staff, negotiated contracts, high transaction volume) that a 5-50 person company doesn't have. Build the four-to-five policy toggles above well, expressed as simple conditions with hard-stop/soft-warn actions, and defer everything else until a customer's size actually demands it.
